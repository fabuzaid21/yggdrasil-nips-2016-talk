A (Brief) Decision Tree Tutorial3

Suppose you are trying to predict whether a home is located in San Francisco or New York.

A decision tree greedily finds a series of decision points in the dataset and recursively splits the data into homogenous partitions.

Decision trees are widely used in machine learning for several reasons:

  1. Arbitrarily expressive: can (theoretically) model any statistical relationship in the data
  2. Easily tunable: only one hyperparameter to tune (max. depth of tree)
  3. Natural feature support: ordinal and categorical features require no pre-processing

Distributed Decision Trees: The Classic Approach (PLANET)

  • Partition training set by row
  • To compute the split for a given node:
    • Each worker computes sufficient statistics on the subset of the training data available to it
    • To limit communication, workers only consider $B$ thresholds, instead of $n+1$
    • The master aggregates the statistics, picks the best feature (and threshold) to split on
  • Communication cost: $O(2^DkpB)$
Notation
\begin{array}{|c|c|} \hline n & \text{# instances} \\ \hline p & \text{# features} \\ \hline D & \text{max. depth of tree} \\ \hline B & \text{# bins to use in PLANET} \\ \hline k & \text{# workers} \\ \hline \end{array}

Distributed Decision Trees: The Classic Approach (PLANET)

Because only $B$ thresholds are considered, optimal split may not always be found, particularly for continuous features!

Key takeaway: Partitioning by row 1) introduces a tradeoff between accuracy and runtime efficiency; and 2) asks the user to tune an additional hyperparameter

The New Approach: Yggdrasil

  • Partition training set by column
  • For each node:
    • Each worker computes sufficient statistics on all of its local features and sends the best feature (and threshold) to the master
    • The master picks the globally best feature to split on from the $k$ candidates
  • Same computational cost as before
  • No $B$ parameter; all thresholds considered
  • Communication cost: $O(2^Dk + Dnk)$
